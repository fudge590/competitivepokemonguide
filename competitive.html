About Competitive Pokemon

Competitive pokemon is complex, and may take a while to learn. You will not be able to put four moves on a pokemon and pick whatever sounds cool, get away with it, and win the battle. Understanding the mechanics of pokemon in battle is crucial to be able to clutch victory from the grasp of the opponent. Every turn, every move you make is of utmost importance to be able to differentiate the good trainers from the best ones. That being said, there are some basics that you can learn before delving into competitive pokemon.

Gameplay mechanics

There are numerous game mechanics that factor into defeating your opponent. By understanding these, we can make the most optimal decision based on the situation that we are in. To defeat your opponent, you have to, of course, knock all their pokemon out by dealing damage to lower their HP to zero. Understanding how to effectively deal damage is one of the simplest yet hardest to master mechanic in pokemon. Since it is a mechanic that we are all familiar with, let's go through that first!

Effectiveness

We all know that fire is super effective against grass. This means that the damage of a fire move against a grass type pokemon factors in the effectiveness of the move, which makes it 2x effective, essentially doubling the damage against the target.
Let's look at another example. Fire is super effective against bug type, which means that fire damage dealt is 2x against a bug type.
However, what happens when a pokemon with two types, grass and bug, is hit by a fire move? The grass/bug pokemon would recieve a whopping 4x damage from the fire move! Type effectiveness is multiplicative, not additive, so since 2 multiplied by 2 is 4, we would say that the grass/bug pokemon is 4x weak to fire. This applies for resistances too. We'll get to that in the next section.

Figure 2.3, A flying type move being used against a fighting type pokemon

A good trick to estimate damage is to multiply the move's power by the type effectiveness, then see if it can kill the pokemon. For example, if you just used Waterfall(80BP) to deal 40% HP regular damage, you essentially can use a move with the same 80BP to deal 80% HP damage to the same pokemon if you deal super effective damage. By the way, BP stands for Base Power. There's a "Terminology" page that you can check out later, but for now I'll make sure it's easy to understand.

Resistances

Resistances work the same way as effectiveness, except that it's just the opposite. It cuts the damage of uneffective attacks either by 1/2 or by 1/4, depending on whether the pokemon is 2x resistant or 4x resistant respectively. Soaking up damage may not be as cool, flashy or exciting as OHKO-ing(One Hit Knock-Out) an opponent's pokemon, but it is vital in making sure that your own pokemon do not suffer that fate.

Stats

Every pokemon has its own stats. Let's take a look at the stats of Charizard, for example. This was sourced from pokemondb.net.

Figure 2.4, Charizard's base stats

Each pokemon has all 6 stats: HP, Atk, Def, SpA, SpD and Speed. These are all abbreviations for the stats, in the same order that the picture shows stats. Each stat has its own unique role in a pokemon, and if you've ever played RPG or MOBA games, you might be familiar with a few of them. If not, fear not, that's why I'm here to explain! :D

HP

HP is probably the simplest stat to understand. A pokemon's HP is just the number of hitpoints it has. For example, a 1000HP pokemon will most likely outlive a 500HP pokemon. There are a few other factors to consider, but for now we can just focus on the HP. A higher HP also means that %HP restoring moves, like Recover, would restore more HP, as Recover will restore 50% of a pokemon's max HP. However, it also means that moves like Giga Drain will absorb more HP from the pokemon, resulting in the pokemon who absorbed HP will have more HP.

Atk/SpA vs Def/SpD

The attack and special-attack stat is where things get fun. We can start by looking at Atk and SpA like Physical and Magic damage. They are both different forms of damage, but they do the same thing: deal damage. If you've ever played an RPG or MOBA game, you'll know that Physical damage targets a player's Armour values, and Magic damage targets Magic Resist values.
In pokemon, Atk targets Def values, and SpA targets SpD values. There are two types of damaging moves in pokemon: Physical and Special. Physical moves use Atk in damage calculation, and Special moves use SpA. Let's take a look at Steelix's stats:

Figure 2.5, Steelix's base stats

Wow! Look at that massive defense stat! Steelix takes Physical hits like a champ. However, since his SpD isn't that high, an appropriate strategy would be to use an attacker who can use Special damage to take Steelix down.

Speed

Speed is the most underrated and unpopular stat for many players looking to transition to competitive pokemon. Many players do not understand how important speed is in closing out games. Speed is the stat that decides who goes first in a turn, simply decided based on which pokemon has a higher speed value.
Although it doesn't sound that important, it actually makes every turn you get have an advantage if you have a high speed. Having a high speed means that few pokemon can outspeed your pokemon. If you manage to boost your offensive stats with a high speed, it means that you are able to constantly OHKO any pokemon that comes into the battlefield, without caring about your defensive stats.

Stat changes

Pokemon's stats can be changed by moves, status effects or through other means, such as abilities. Stat changes play a crucial role in competitive pokemon, providing counterplay. At the same time, if you play around the possible counterplay, you will be able to set up your own pokemon for some big plays.

Stat changing terminology

Each pokemon can be stat boosted in each stat(except HP) up to 6 stacks(also called stages). Each stack contributes 0.5x of a stat, and we call adding a stack +1. For example, a pokemon with +1 Atk will have 1.5x Atk, a pokemon with +2 Atk will have 2x Atk. A pokemon with +6 Atk at max stacks will have +6 Atk.
The only exceptions to this would be for evasion and accuracy, where each stack only contributes 0.33x of each stat.

Same Type Attack Bonus(STAB)

When using a move that is the same type as its holder, the move's power gets multiplied by 1.5x! Surprisingly, more people than you'd expect do not know this. STAB is actually a very important factor in calculating damage, especially if it is neutral damage. The move with the higher power does not always deal the most damage, so do factor STAB in when estimating damage!

EVs and IVs

A pokemon's base stats can be naturally improved through EVs and IVs. IVs are automatically generated on birth, so we don't really have to worry about them as in competitive, we can set them as the highest value, 31 for each stat. However, managing EVs are when it starts to get interesting. A pokemon can achieve a maximum of 508 EVs, and can invest a maximum of 252 EVs in each stat.

Natures

Every pokemon has access to all natures. A nature is a personality trait of a pokemon(adamant, modest, jolly etc.) that raises one of the stats of the pokemon by 10%, but lowers another stat by 10%. Let's take a look at the Garchomp from before.

Figure 2.9, Garchomp's EV and Adamant nature invested stats

Just look at the difference. Garchomp now has a whopping 394 Atk! Compare that to our uninvested and invested stats, and you'll see just how much of a difference EVs and nature can make.
In this case, we used an Adamant nature on the garchomp(+Atk -SpA) to maximise the use of our nature. Since garchomp is a physical attacker because of its high Atk stat, we should only teach it physical moves. Without the use of any special move, our garchomp's SpA simply doesn't matter because it will not be used for anything at all.

Status effects

All pokemon can be afflicted with status effects. However, some are immune to their own kind of status effects. For example, an electric type cannot be paralysed, a poison type cannot be poisoned, a grass type cannot be afflicted by powder moves etc.

Figure 2.10, Bibarel inflicted with poison

There are two types of status effects: volatile and non volatile.

Volatile status effects

Volatile status effects will go away after a while, and do not count towards moves like Hex, where it has different effects if the target is afflicted with a non-volatile(major) status effect. Some examples of volatile status effects include:

Confusion. A confused pokemon has a 33% chance to hurt itself instead of executing the selected move.
Flinch. A pokemon who flinches cannot do anything.
Taunt. A taunted pokemon cannot execute non-damaging moves like recover, toxic etc.

Non-volatile(major) status effects

Non-volatile status effects are what most of us are familiar with. Poison, burn, paralysis are some examples and their effects are very obvious and easy to understand. However, there are some hidden effects that status effects have that we may not know about. For example:

Burn removes 1/8 max HP per turn, but also halves Atk stat. This includes all stat changes, so if a pokemon has been boosted to 1000Atk, the pokemon will have 500Atk if it is burnt.
Paralysis gives a 25% chance of being paralyzed, but also halves Speed stat. It has the same logic as burn, so it halves the post-modifier Speed stat.

Since non-volatile status effects still persist even when the pokemon is switched out, it is a great way to hinder strong physical attackers in the opponent's team and render fast, annoying pokemon vulnerable to attacks.

Some other great status effects to take note of are:

Poison removes 1/8 max HP per turn.
Badly poisoned pokemon get 1/16 max HP removed on the first turn, but it increases by 1/16 max HP on subsequent turns, per turn. This means that on turn 1, 1/16 max HP is removed. On turn 2, 2/16(which is 1/8) max HP is removed. On turn 3, 3/16 max HP is removed etc.
